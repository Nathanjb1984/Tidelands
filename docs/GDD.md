# 2. HIGH-CONCEPT PITCH

> **TIDELINES** is a premium 16-bit strategy game where you lead a Haida chiefdom across generations — commanding great canoes across dangerous Pacific waters, building a thriving village of longhouses and monumental poles, hosting legendary potlatches to convert wealth into undying prestige, forging clan alliances through marriage, and navigating the tides of history from an era of flourishing to the pressures of colonial disruption.
>
> It plays like nothing else: part *Civilization* on the ocean, part *Oregon Trail* in a war canoe, part *Banished* with ceremonial purpose, part *Crusader Kings* with matrilineal descent — all rendered in stunning 16-bit pixel art inspired by the misty, magnificent Pacific Northwest coast.
>
> The sea is your highway. Canoes are your most prized possessions. Generosity — not hoarding — is the path to power. Your poles record the only history that matters: yours.
>
> This is not a battle game. This is not a city builder. This is the story of a people carried through time on cedar and salt water.

# 3. CORE FANTASY STATEMENT

You are a chief — not a king, not a general, not a CEO. You are the living node of a vast web of kinship, obligation, ocean knowledge, and inherited responsibility. Your canoe carries your people's future across waters that can kill without warning. Your longhouse shelters a lineage stretching back beyond memory. Your potlatch table proves your worth not by what you keep, but by what you can afford to give away. Your monumental poles will stand long after your name is spoken — carved witnesses to the choices you made when the fog rolled in and the sea demanded an answer. In **Tidelines**, every paddle stroke matters, every alliance echoes across generations, every pole tells a story, and the ocean never stops testing whether you are worthy of calling yourself its master.

# 4. GAMEPLAY PILLARS

### Pillar 1: THE SEA IS THE WORLD
The ocean is not background decoration. It is the primary game space — the highway, the battlefield, the provider, and the existential threat. Every major decision in the game connects to the water: travel routes, fishing grounds, weather windows, tidal timing, naval presence, and the sheer grandeur and terror of the open Pacific. The player should *feel* the ocean in every session.

**Design Implications:**
- Canoe navigation is a dedicated, skill-expressive gameplay mode
- Weather, tides, currents, and fog are simulated and meaningful
- The world map is primarily coastal/marine — islands, inlets, straits, open water
- The most dramatic moments happen on the water

### Pillar 2: CANOES ARE SACRED TECHNOLOGY
Canoes are not generic transport units. They are the most important material objects in the game — each one a work of engineering, artistry, and spiritual investment. Different canoe classes serve different purposes, and building a great canoe should feel as significant as constructing a wonder in Civilization.

**Design Implications:**
- 5+ canoe classes with distinct stats, roles, and visual profiles
- Canoe construction is a multi-step, resource-intensive process
- Canoe handling has skill expression (paddling cadence, wave reading, current use)
- Named canoes with history, reputation, and blessing status

### Pillar 3: GENEROSITY IS POWER
The potlatch economy inverts the hoarding instinct of most strategy games. Accumulating wealth is necessary — but only as a means to redistribute it. Prestige, political power, alliance strength, and legacy scoring all flow from what the player gives away, not what they keep. This single inversion makes Tidelines feel unlike any other strategy game.

**Design Implications:**
- Dual-track economy: material wealth vs. prestige
- Potlatch preparation is a major mid-to-late game loop
- Hoarding wealth without redistribution causes social/political penalties
- The most powerful endgame states require massive generosity events

### Pillar 4: LINEAGE IS DESTINY
Identity flows through the mother's line. Raven and Eagle moieties shape diplomacy, marriage, ceremony, and succession. The player is never just "a leader" — they are a specific person in a specific web of kinship, and their choices ripple through that web across generations.

**Design Implications:**
- Matrilineal inheritance and moiety identity affect all social systems
- Marriage is a strategic alliance tool with lasting consequences
- Succession crises and lineage events create emergent drama
- The player periodically transitions to a new chief (generational play)

### Pillar 5: POLES RECORD HISTORY
Monumental poles are not decorations. They are the game's memory system — permanent, visual, meaningful records of what happened during play. Raising a pole is one of the most significant acts in the game, requiring resources, skilled labor, ceremony, and a story worth telling. Poles change the village visually, boost prestige permanently, and define what "your game" looked like versus someone else's.

**Design Implications:**
- Pole commissioning involves choosing crests, themes, and commemorations
- Carving takes real game-time and requires master carvers
- Pole-raising is a ceremony with guests, feasting, and prestige effects
- Poles are visible in the village and referenced in the legacy endgame score

### Pillar 6: THE WORLD CHANGES
History is not static. The game progresses through eras — from flourishing pre-contact maritime civilization through expanding influence, into the pressures of epidemic, colonial encroachment, and forced change. The player must adapt their strategy as the world shifts beneath them, and the emotional tone of the game changes dramatically across its arc.

**Design Implications:**
- Era transitions alter available actions, events, threats, and tone
- Later eras introduce historical pressures that cannot be "beaten" in a simple sense
- The endgame is about resilience, memory, and what endures — not total domination
- Music, palette, and event systems shift with era

### Pillar 7: BEAUTY IS NOT OPTIONAL
This game must be gorgeous. The Pacific Northwest coast — its fog, its cedar forests, its violent surf, its quiet coves, its firelit longhouses, its carved poles against grey sky — is one of the most visually dramatic settings in the world. The 16-bit art must capture this atmosphere with the care and density of the best SNES-era pixel art. No generic tiles. No flat palettes. No sterile educational software aesthetic.

**Design Implications:**
- Custom parallax environments for every biome/weather/time combination
- Dedicated animation frames for canoe handling, ceremonies, carving, storms
- Palette shifts for dawn/dusk/storm/fog/firelight
- UI is pixel-art-native with iconography inspired by formline design principles

### Pillar 8: RESPECT IS THE FOUNDATION
Every system in this game touches on real cultural practices, real history, and real identity. The design must be reverent without being stiff, atmospheric without being exploitative, and engaging without being trivializing. Spiritual systems feel like guidance and mystery, not cartoon power-ups. Conflict is strategic and consequential, not gleeful. Cultural practices are presented with weight and dignity.

**Design Implications:**
- No "magic spell" framing for spiritual systems
- No graphic violence or exploitation
- Advisory/consultation process recommended during production
- Tone review as a formal milestone at each development phase

# 5. CORE LOOP

## Minute-to-Minute (Session Gameplay)

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    VILLAGE PHASE                      │
│  Assign labor → Check resources → Plan next action   │
│  Start carving / building / food prep                │
└──────────────────────┬──────────────────────────────┘
                       │
                       ▼
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                   VOYAGE PHASE                       │
│  Choose destination → Load canoe → Launch            │
│  Navigate tides/weather → Encounter events           │
│  Fish / Trade / Explore / Raid / Diplomacy           │
└──────────────────────┬──────────────────────────────┘
                       │
                       ▼
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                   RETURN PHASE                       │
│  Unload cargo → Process results → Advance time       │
│  Check village growth → Resolve events               │
└──────────────────────┬──────────────────────────────┘
                       │
                       ▼
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                  CEREMONY PHASE                      │
│  (Seasonal / triggered by accumulation)              │
│  Prepare feast → Invite guests → Host potlatch       │
│  Raise pole → Celebrate alliance → Mark legacy       │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Typical 15-minute session:**
1. **Dawn at the village** — check food stores, assign people. A master carver reports progress on a memorial pole. Rain is clearing.
2. **Load the trade canoe** — eulachon oil, dried halibut, woven blankets. Destination: a Tsimshian village two days north. Bring an Eagle-clan nephew to strengthen the marriage alliance.
3. **Navigate** — paddle out of the inlet, read the current, choose the inside passage (safer, slower) or the open strait (faster, riskier with that swell). A fog bank rolls in — use the raven omen from yesterday's dream to guide you through.
4. **Arrive and trade** — exchange goods, negotiate, hear news of raiders to the south. Secure a copper. Begin the return.
5. **Homecoming** — unload goods, distribute food, advance the season. The pole is nearly finished. Start gathering provisions for the raising ceremony next moon.

## Run-to-Run (Multi-Session Arc)

Each "run" covers a chief's lifetime (roughly 15-30 game-years, playable across 4-8 hours). Within a single chieftainship:

- **Early Chieftainship:** Establish yourself. Build your first significant canoe. Secure a reliable food economy. Make your first alliance.
- **Middle Chieftainship:** Expand trade routes. Commission a pole. Navigate a conflict. Host your first major potlatch.
- **Late Chieftainship:** Secure succession. Build your legacy. Make defining choices about war, diplomacy, or ceremony. Host the great potlatch.
- **Transition:** Your chief passes. The new chief inherits — some advantages, some debts, some unfinished work. Some allies remain loyal. Some do not.

## Campaign-Long Loop (Full Game Arc)

The full campaign spans 5 eras across 3-5 generations of chiefs (30-60 hours total):

```
Era 1: Flourishing    → Build foundation, learn systems
Era 2: Expansion      → Extend reach, deepen alliances
Era 3: Great Legacy   → Peak power, defining moments
Era 4: Disruption     → Survive historical pressures
Era 5: Enduring Memory → What remains? Legacy scoring.
```

The campaign loop:
**Build → Travel → Risk → Return → Prepare → Give → Commemorate → Repeat across generations → Final reckoning of what endures.**

# 6. FULL FEATURE SET

## 6.1 — Canoe Navigation System

### Canoe Classes

| Class | Crew | Cargo | Speed | Seaworthiness | Role |
|-------|------|-------|-------|---------------|------|
| **Fishing Canoe** | 2-4 | Low | Medium | Medium | Daily harvest, short trips |
| **Travel Canoe** | 6-10 | Medium | High | High | Long-distance voyages, exploration |
| **Trade Canoe** | 8-14 | Very High | Medium | High | Bulk cargo, trade missions |
| **War Canoe** | 12-20 | Low | High | Very High | Raids, defense, intimidation |
| **Prestige Canoe** | 16-24 | Medium | Medium | Very High | Ceremony, diplomacy, potlatch arrivals |

### Navigation Mechanics
- **Paddling Cadence:** Rhythmic input affects speed and stamina. Skilled rhythm = faster, more efficient travel.
- **Current Reading:** Visible current indicators on the map. Traveling with/against currents significantly affects speed and risk.
- **Tidal Windows:** Some passages are only safe at certain tide levels. Timing departures matters.
- **Weather Assessment:** Pre-voyage weather reading (spiritual/observational) gives partial forecast. Unexpected weather is a major risk.
- **Fog Navigation:** Reduced visibility. Landmark-based navigation. Omen guidance helps.
- **Shore Hugging vs. Open Water:** Inside passages are slower but safer. Open crossings are faster but expose the canoe to swells, wind, and disorientation.
- **Beaching:** Landing requires suitable beaches. Rocky shores, heavy surf, and unfamiliar coastlines add risk.
- **Canoe Naming:** Each canoe can be named. Named canoes accumulate voyage history and prestige.

### Canoe Construction
1. **Select cedar log** — requires access to old-growth cedar (increasingly scarce in later eras)
2. **Rough shaping** — labor-intensive, requires skilled workers
3. **Steam bending** — specialist knowledge, risk of failure
4. **Carving and finishing** — decorative prow, painted designs, crest imagery
5. **Blessing ceremony** — spiritual preparation before first launch
6. **Launch** — event with prestige effects

### Canoe Upgrades
- Reinforced hull (seaworthiness)
- Extended gunwales (cargo capacity)
- Streamlined prow (speed)
- Crest prow carving (intimidation / prestige)
- Blessed hull (spiritual protection modifier)
- Veteran crew bonus (accumulated from successful voyages)

## 6.2 — Tides / Weather / Sea Hazard System

### Tidal Cycle
- 4-phase tide per game-day: high → falling → low → rising
- Certain channels only passable at high tide
- Exposed reefs and sandbars at low tide (fishing opportunity AND navigation hazard)
- Tidal currents in narrows (speed bonus or deadly risk)

### Weather System
- **Clear:** Full visibility, normal travel
- **Overcast:** Reduced visibility at distance, normal travel
- **Rain:** Reduced visibility, slower travel, crew morale drain
- **Heavy Rain:** Significantly reduced visibility, rough water in open areas
- **Fog:** Very low visibility, disorientation risk, navigation check required
- **Storm:** Dangerous. High waves, wind, capsizing risk. Only high-seaworthiness canoes should attempt.
- **Gale:** Lethal in open water. Seek shelter immediately.

Weather patterns are semi-random but seasonally weighted:
- Spring: Variable, rain common, fog frequent
- Summer: Best weather windows, occasional storms
- Autumn: Increasingly stormy, shorter good-weather windows
- Winter: Storms frequent, travel extremely dangerous, home-season

### Sea Hazards
- **Reefs:** Visible at low tide, submerged at high. Damage or wreck canoe.
- **Riptides:** Strong localized currents. Can pull canoe off course.
- **Whirlpools/Tidal races:** In narrow passages. Timing-dependent danger.
- **Floating debris:** Storm aftermath. Minor damage risk.
- **Open-water swells:** Gradual danger increase far from shore.
- **Capsizing risk:** Based on canoe seaworthiness vs. sea state. Crew/cargo loss.

## 6.3 — Village Economy

### Resources

| Resource | Source | Use |
|----------|--------|-----|
| **Food (salmon)** | Fishing, seasonal runs | Primary sustenance, potlatch feasting |
| **Food (halibut)** | Deep-water fishing | High-value food, trade good |
| **Food (eulachon oil)** | Seasonal harvest | Critical trade good, preservative |
| **Food (berries/roots)** | Gathering | Supplemental food, medicine |
| **Food (shellfish)** | Tidal gathering | Reliable low-effort food |
| **Cedar (logs)** | Forest harvesting | Canoes, houses, poles, boxes |
| **Cedar (bark)** | Bark stripping | Weaving, rope, clothing |
| **Hides / Furs** | Hunting, trade | Clothing, trade goods, ceremony |
| **Stone / Bone** | Gathering, trade | Tools (early eras) |
| **Copper** | Trade (extremely rare) | Supreme prestige object |
| **Woven Goods** | Weaving labor | Trade, ceremony, potlatch gifts |
| **Carved Objects** | Carving labor | Trade, ceremony, potlatch gifts, prestige |

### Labor System
Village population divided into roles:
- **Fishers / Harvesters:** Food production
- **Canoe Builders:** Long-term canoe construction projects
- **Carvers:** Poles, boxes, masks, decorative items
- **Weavers:** Bark processing, blanket weaving
- **Warriors / Scouts:** Defense, expedition crew, patrol
- **Ceremonial Specialists:** Spiritual guidance, ceremony preparation
- **Youth / Apprentices:** Lower skill, growing into roles

Labor assignment is seasonal:
- **Spring:** Prepare canoes, early fishing, gather bark
- **Summer:** Peak fishing (salmon runs), long voyages, trade
- **Autumn:** Harvest, preserve food, prepare for winter, build
- **Winter:** Carving, weaving, ceremony, potlatch season, stories, planning

### Food Economy
- **Surplus target:** The village must accumulate enough preserved food for winter AND ceremonial obligations
- **Preservation methods:** Smoking, drying, oil rendering — requires labor and facilities
- **Feast obligations:** Potlatch and ceremony require massive food surplus
- **Starvation risk:** Failed harvests or raids on stores = demographic crisis

## 6.4 — Potlatch Prestige Economy

*See Section 9 for full system design.*

Core concept: **Two parallel economies run simultaneously.**

| Material Economy | Prestige Economy |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| Food stores | Honor rating |
| Goods inventory | Generosity reputation |
| Canoe fleet | Alliance strength |
| Village buildings | Ceremony memory |
| Trade surplus | Legacy score |
| Copper holdings | Pole count / quality |

Material wealth feeds into prestige through **redistribution events** (potlatch).  
Prestige cannot be gained solely through accumulation — it requires giving.

## 6.5 — Clan and Lineage System

*See Section 11 for full system design.*

Key mechanics:
- Every character belongs to Raven or Eagle moiety (matrilineal)
- Marriage must cross moiety lines (Raven marries Eagle and vice versa)
- Children inherit mother's moiety and clan
- Succession follows matrilineal logic (nephew via sister is a common heir)
- Moiety identity affects available crests, ceremonies, alliances, and bonuses
- Clan relationships create a web of obligation and opportunity

## 6.6 — Marriage Alliance System

- Marriage proposals are diplomatic actions
- Cross-moiety requirement creates strategic pairing constraints
- Successful marriages strengthen trade and political ties with spouse's community
- Children born into marriages carry both lineage connections
- High-prestige marriages require significant bride-wealth exchange
- Marriage alliances can prevent conflict, open trade routes, or secure succession

## 6.7 — Pole Planning and Carving System

*See Section 10 for full system design.*

Summary:
- Commission a pole → choose type and theme → select crests → assign carver(s) → gather cedar → carving progresses over time → ceremony planning → unveiling event → permanent village feature

## 6.8 — Expedition Loadout System

Before any voyage, the player must:
1. **Choose canoe** — type, condition, crew capacity
2. **Select crew** — number, skill level, named characters with traits
3. **Load cargo** — trade goods, food for journey, gifts, weapons if needed
4. **Set destination** — known or exploratory
5. **Choose route** — inside passage vs. open water, stopping points
6. **Check conditions** — season, weather forecast, spiritual reading
7. **Receive blessing** — optional but provides spiritual modifier
8. **Launch** — committed. Cannot easily turn back once in open water.

Loadout decisions create meaningful trade-offs:
- More cargo = slower, lower freeboard, more capsizing risk
- More warriors = less room for trade goods
- Faster canoe = less cargo capacity
- Longer route = more food needed, more encounters
- Spiritual blessing = costs ceremony resources but reduces risk

## 6.9 — Tactical Conflict System

*See Section 13 for full system design.*

Summary: Phase-based strategic resolution. Pre-battle planning → approach → engagement → resolution. Morale, intimidation, weather, crew quality, and canoe type all matter. Non-graphic. Consequences are political and material, not gore.

## 6.10 — Spiritual Omen / Blessing System

*See Section 12 for full system design.*

Summary: Omens appear as events — animal sightings, dream sequences, weather signs. The player interprets them (sometimes with advisor help) to modify expedition decisions. Blessings provide buffs for specific undertakings. The system is atmospheric, not mechanical min-maxing.

## 6.11 — Historical Era Transition System

*See Section 14 for full campaign design.*

Each era transition is triggered by reaching certain thresholds (prestige, time, events). Transitions involve:
- Narrative interlude with cinematic text/art
- Shift in available events and threats
- Possible demographic changes
- Music and palette shifts
- New systems or system modifications unlocking

## 6.12 — Chief Legacy / Succession System

When a chief dies or retires:
1. **Legacy Assessment:** Calculate achievements — poles raised, potlatches hosted, alliances made, voyages completed, conflicts survived
2. **Succession Selection:** Choose from eligible heirs (matrilineal preference). Each heir has different traits, alliances, and starting conditions.
3. **Inheritance:** New chief receives village, canoes, some prestige, some alliances — but NOT all. Some allies drift. Some rivals see opportunity. Some resources were given away at the funeral potlatch.
4. **Fresh Start Feeling:** Each new chief should feel like a partial reset — you inherit a world, but must prove yourself anew.

# 7. WORLD / MAP DESIGN

## Map Structure

The game world represents a fictionalized but geographically faithful version of the Haida Gwaii archipelago and surrounding coastal region. The map is divided into zones:

### Home Waters (Starting Zone)
- **Your village inlet** — protected bay, good beach, defensible
- **Nearby fishing grounds** — salmon streams, halibut banks, shellfish beaches
- **Cedar forests** — accessible old-growth stands (depleting over eras)
- **Neighboring villages** — 2-3 other communities within half-day paddle

### Inner Archipelago
- **Island chain** — dozens of islands with varied resources
- **Protected channels** — safe-ish travel routes between islands
- **Sacred sites** — spiritually significant locations, omen events
- **Hidden coves** — shelter from storms, possible cache sites
- **Seasonal camps** — temporary fishing/harvesting stations

### Outer Coast
- **Exposed Pacific shoreline** — dangerous but resource-rich
- **Major fishing grounds** — halibut banks far offshore
- **Nesting colonies** — seabird eggs (seasonal)
- **Rocky headlands** — navigation hazards, dramatic scenery
- **Kelp forests** — navigation aid and marine harvest

### The Strait (Mid-range Travel)
- **Open water crossing** — the most dangerous regular voyage
- **Mainland coast access** — Tsimshian, Tlingit, and other trading partners
- **River mouths** — eulachon runs, trade meeting points
- **Contested waters** — overlapping territorial claims

### Distant Waters (Long-range Expeditions)
- **Northern routes** — Tlingit territory, different goods
- **Southern routes** — Coast Salish and Nuu-chah-nulth trading networks
- **Interior river routes** — access to inland trade goods (rare)
- **Legendary destinations** — extremely difficult voyages with unique rewards

## Points of Interest

- **Trading posts** — established exchange points with allied or neutral groups
- **Salmon streams** — seasonal abundance, competition
- **Cedar groves** — large trees for canoe logs, increasingly contested
- **Reef gardens** — managed clam/fish habitats
- **Burial islands** — respectful avoidance; spiritual events if disturbed
- **Whale migration routes** — seasonal encounter opportunities
- **Storm shelter coves** — critical emergency refuges
- **Ancient village sites** — exploration events, historical memory
- **Copper sources** — extremely rare and distant trade connections

## Navigation Features

- **Current arrows** — visible on map, shift with tide
- **Fog zones** — roll in dynamically, obscure map portions
- **Weather fronts** — visible approaching storm systems
- **Landmark system** — notable headlands, rocks, and islands that aid navigation
- **Route memory** — previously traveled routes become faster/safer
- **Seasonal overlays** — salmon run timing, weather patterns, resource availability shift

# 8. VILLAGE DESIGN

## Village Layout

The village sits along a beach in a protected inlet. Buildings face the water. The forest rises behind. The sky and sea dominate the view.

### Core Structures

**Longhouse (Chief's House)**
- Central structure, largest building
- Houses the chief's extended family
- Hosts ceremonies and feasts
- Interior scenes for key story moments
- Upgradeable: size, interior quality, fire pit, storage

**Secondary Longhouses**
- House other families / lineage branches
- Population capacity
- Can be expanded or added as village grows
- Each associated with a family and their labor

**Canoe House**
- Shelters canoes under construction and in storage
- Work site for canoe building
- Upgradeable: capacity, tool quality
- Visual: canoes visible inside, workers active

**Smoke House**
- Fish preservation facility
- Converts fresh fish to preserved food (critical for winter and potlatch)
- Capacity determines preservation rate
- Seasonal bottleneck if undersized

**Carving Shed**
- Where poles, masks, boxes, and decorative items are created
- Requires assigned carvers
- Tool quality affects output
- Master carver NPC may be recruited or trained

**Weaving House**
- Cedar bark processing and blanket/textile production
- Produces trade goods, ceremonial items, potlatch gifts
- Steady background production

**Food Cache / Storage**
- Preserved food and valuable goods storage
- Size determines maximum stockpile
- Vulnerable to raid if village is attacked
- Fire risk (random event)

**Ceremonial Ground**
- Open area before the chief's house
- Where potlatch, pole-raising, and ceremonies take place
- Visually transforms during events: fire, crowd, decorations
- Permanent poles stand here after raising

**Beach / Landing**
- Where canoes are launched and beached
- Visual indicator of fleet size
- Lookout post can be built here (early warning)

**Watchtower / Lookout**
- Optional defensive structure
- Early warning of approaching canoes
- Defensive bonus during raids

### Village Visual Growth

The village should visually transform across the game:

**Early Village (Era 1):**
- 2-3 longhouses, basic canoe house
- 1-2 canoes on beach
- Simple smoke house
- Bare ceremonial ground

**Established Village (Era 2-3):**
- 5-7 longhouses, large canoe house
- Multiple canoes, including a war canoe
- Active carving shed with pole in progress
- 1-3 standing poles
- Larger food caches
- Smoke from multiple houses

**Great Village (Era 3 peak):**
- 8-10+ longhouses, major canoe house
- Fleet of diverse canoes
- Multiple standing poles (each unique, based on player choices)
- Impressive ceremonial ground
- Wealthy, active, visually dense

**Disrupted Village (Era 4):**
- Some houses empty or deteriorating
- Fewer people visible
- Some poles still standing, some weathering
- Quieter, more somber atmosphere
- Visual storytelling through absence

# 9. POTLATCH SYSTEM

## Overview

The potlatch is the game's central prestige mechanism and one of its defining systems. In Tidelines, wealth is not power until it is given away. The potlatch converts accumulated material surplus into:
- **Prestige** (permanent reputation score)
- **Political power** (alliance strengthening, rival submission)
- **Social obligation** (guests owe return hospitality)
- **Legacy score** (endgame memory)
- **Specific diplomatic outcomes** (peace, marriage, succession legitimacy)

## Potlatch Types

### 1. Feast Potlatch (Small)
- **Purpose:** Seasonal celebration, minor prestige maintenance
- **Scale:** Local guests, your village + 1-2 neighbors
- **Cost:** Moderate food, minor gift distribution
- **Prestige Gain:** Low-medium
- **Frequency:** Can be held every 1-2 seasons

### 2. Name-Giving Potlatch
- **Purpose:** Bestow hereditary names, validate a new chief, honor lineage
- **Scale:** Regional guests from your moiety network
- **Cost:** Significant food and gifts
- **Prestige Gain:** Medium-high
- **Special Effect:** Legitimizes succession, strengthens lineage identity

### 3. Pole-Raising Potlatch
- **Purpose:** Unveil a completed monumental pole
- **Scale:** Major regional event, all significant neighbors invited
- **Cost:** Very high food, major gift redistribution, pole construction cost
- **Prestige Gain:** Very high
- **Special Effect:** Permanent pole added to village, permanently recorded in legacy

### 4. Rivalry Potlatch
- **Purpose:** Challenge a rival's prestige, establish dominance
- **Scale:** Targeted rival + witnesses
- **Cost:** Extreme — the point is to give away more than the rival can match
- **Prestige Gain:** Enormous if successful
- **Risk:** If rival matches or exceeds your generosity, you lose prestige
- **Special Mechanics:** Copper breaking (destroying a copper to show contempt for its value)

### 5. Memorial Potlatch
- **Purpose:** Honor a deceased chief, complete mourning obligations
- **Scale:** Large, all allied communities
- **Cost:** High — includes redistributing the deceased chief's personal property
- **Prestige Gain:** High
- **Special Effect:** Required for clean succession. Skipping damages legitimacy.

### 6. Peace Potlatch
- **Purpose:** End a conflict, establish peace terms
- **Scale:** Both conflicting parties + neutral witnesses
- **Cost:** Very high (both sides contribute, but host bears more)
- **Prestige Gain:** Medium-high
- **Special Effect:** Conflict resolution, possible marriage alliance

## Potlatch Preparation Loop

1. **Declare Intent** — Choose potlatch type. Set a target date (season).
2. **Accumulate Wealth** — Spend multiple seasons gathering:
   - Surplus preserved food (salmon, halibut, eulachon oil)
   - Woven blankets and cedar bark goods
   - Carved items (boxes, bowls, masks)
   - Trade goods collected from voyages
   - Coppers (for the most prestigious events)
3. **Prepare the Feast Space** — Ensure ceremonial ground is ready. Decorate.
4. **Send Invitations** — Dispatch canoes to invite guests. Guest list affects outcomes.
   - Inviting more/higher-prestige guests = more prestige if successful
   - Failing to invite someone important = political slight
   - Inviting a rival = risky but high-reward
5. **Host the Event** — Multi-phase ceremony:
   - **Arrival:** Guests arrive by canoe (impressive arrivals from high-prestige guests)
   - **Feasting:** Food distribution. Quality and quantity matter.
   - **Speeches/Performance:** Chief speaks, dances performed, stories told
   - **Gift Distribution:** The core act. Player chooses how to distribute gifts among guests.
   - **Outcome:** Prestige calculated based on scale, quality, and social impact.

## Gift Distribution Strategy

During the potlatch, the player allocates gifts to specific guests or groups:
- **Give generously to allies** → strengthens alliance
- **Give lavishly to rivals** → demonstrates superiority (they must reciprocate or lose face)
- **Give to neutral parties** → opens new diplomatic channels
- **Give to the poor/low-status** → earns broad community loyalty
- **Withhold from someone** → deliberate slight (politically aggressive)

## Prestige Calculation

```
Base Prestige = (Total Gift Value) × (Guest Prestige Multiplier)
Bonus: +% for pole-raising potlatch
Bonus: +% for copper redistribution or breaking
Bonus: +% for unprecedented scale
Penalty: -% if food quality is poor
Penalty: -% if important guests were slighted
Penalty: -% if rival's return potlatch exceeds yours within 2 years
```

## Long-Term Potlatch Effects

- **Social Obligation Network:** Guests are now in your debt. They must eventually reciprocate.
- **Alliance Maintenance:** Regular potlatch hosting keeps alliances active. Neglect causes drift.
- **Reputation Threshold Effects:** At high prestige levels, new diplomatic options open, rivals back down more easily, and more people want to marry into your lineage.
- **Legacy Score:** Potlatches hosted are a major component of the endgame legacy calculation.

# 10. POLE SYSTEM

## Overview

Monumental poles are the game's most powerful legacy tool. Each pole is unique, generated by player choices, and serves as a permanent record of what happened in your specific playthrough. No two games should produce the same poles.

## Pole Types

### 1. House Frontal Pole
- Stands before the chief's longhouse
- Displays lineage crests and family identity
- **Effect:** Village prestige aura, lineage legitimacy
- **When:** Early-midgame, after establishing village identity

### 2. Memorial Pole
- Honors a deceased chief or important person
- Crests reflect the honored individual's life achievements
- **Effect:** Mourning completion, succession legitimacy, ongoing prestige
- **When:** After a chief's death, paired with memorial potlatch

### 3. History Pole
- Commemorates a major event: battle victory, great voyage, alliance
- Crests and figures narrate the specific event
- **Effect:** Permanent prestige boost, historical record
- **When:** After significant achievements

### 4. Shame Pole (Rare)
- Erected to publicly shame a rival who failed to honor obligations
- Inverted or distorted figure of the shamed party
- **Effect:** Major prestige damage to target, diplomatic consequences
- **When:** Rare, high-risk political act

### 5. Welcome Pole
- Stands at the beach/landing area
- Identifies the village and chief to arriving visitors
- **Effect:** First-impression bonus for diplomacy, trade premium
- **When:** Midgame, after village is established

## Pole Creation Process

### Step 1: Commission
- Player selects pole type and general theme
- Choose what is commemorated (from a list of eligible achievements/events)
- Assign a master carver (named NPC with skill rating)

### Step 2: Design
- **Select crests:** Choose from available crest figures based on moiety and lineage:
  - Raven, Eagle, Killer Whale, Bear, Frog, Wolf, Thunderbird, Shark, etc.
- **Choose arrangement:** Top-to-bottom figure order (affects visual and meaning)
- **Select embellishments:** Wings spread, figures holding objects, additional small figures
- **Name the pole:** Optional, adds narrative flavor

### Step 3: Materials
- Requires a large red cedar log (special resource, must be harvested or traded for)
- Quality of cedar affects carving time and visual quality
- Transportation of the log to the village is an event

### Step 4: Carving
- Takes multiple game-seasons (varies by pole size and carver skill)
- Progress is visible in the carving shed
- Random events during carving: carver has vision (adds bonus detail), tool breaks (delay), apprentice learns (trains next generation)
- Player can visit and see work-in-progress

### Step 5: Painting
- After carving, the pole is painted
- Color choices affect visual appearance in the village
- Traditional colors: red, black, blue-green (reflecting real formline art practice)

### Step 6: Raising Ceremony
- Major event — requires potlatch-level preparation
- Guests invited to witness
- Ropes, labor, coordinated effort to raise the pole (cinematic sequence)
- Speeches, feasting, gift-giving
- The moment the pole stands upright should be one of the most emotionally impactful moments in the game

### Step 7: Standing
- The pole appears permanently in the village view
- Its unique design (based on player choices) is visible
- Hovering/selecting it shows its history, crests, and the event it commemorates
- Over time (especially in later eras), poles weather — paint fades, wood greys
- The endgame references which poles still stand and what they recorded

## Pole Visual Generation

The game uses a modular sprite system to generate unique poles:
- Base column sprite (height based on pole size)
- Stackable crest figure modules (each figure is a sprite block)
- Wing/arm extensions
- Paint color overlays
- Weathering shader for older poles

This means every pole the player creates looks distinct in the village, and a late-game village with 5-6 poles has a completely unique skyline.

# 11. LINEAGE / CLAN SYSTEM

## Moiety Structure

All characters in the game belong to one of two moieties:
- **Raven** (Yáahl)
- **Eagle** (Ts'áak — which includes some groups also associated with Wolf)

### Rules
- Moiety is inherited from the **mother**
- Marriage must be **exogamous** — Raven marries Eagle, Eagle marries Raven. Always.
- Children belong to the **mother's moiety**
- The chief's heir is typically the **sister's son** (matrilineal nephew), not the chief's own son

### Gameplay Impact of Moiety

**Raven Moiety Bonuses:**
- Trickster's Fortune: Small chance of turning bad events into opportunities
- Clever Diplomacy: +bonus to trade negotiations
- Adaptive Navigation: Better fog navigation
- Reversal Omen: Access to "sudden reversal" omen cards
- Associated Crests: Raven, Frog, Beaver, Bear (some)

**Eagle Moiety Bonuses:**
- Keen Sight: Better scouting and early warning
- Commanding Presence: +bonus to intimidation and potlatch prestige
- Wind Favor: Sailing speed bonus in favorable conditions
- Leadership Omen: Access to "clarity of purpose" omen cards
- Associated Crests: Eagle, Killer Whale, Wolf, Shark, Thunderbird (some)

## Clan Sub-Groups

Within each moiety, several clans exist. Each clan has:
- Specific crest rights (determines which pole figures they can use)
- Traditional territories and resource access
- Historical alliances and rivalries
- Unique clan-specific events

## Marriage System

### Proposing Marriage
1. Identify a potential spouse from the opposite moiety
2. Evaluate their family's prestige, territory, and connections
3. Negotiate bride-wealth (goods exchanged from groom's family to bride's)
4. If accepted, marriage creates ongoing alliance

### Marriage Effects
- **Trade route bonus** with spouse's community
- **Diplomatic immunity** — reduced conflict risk with in-law community
- **Children** — next generation with combined lineage connections
- **Obligation** — expected to support spouse's family in potlatch and conflict
- **Succession pool** — children may become eligible heirs in either community

### Strategic Marriage Considerations
- Marrying into a wealthy family increases trade access but creates obligations
- Marrying into a distant community opens new routes but strains logistics
- Multiple marriages across the family network create a web of alliances
- Breaking a marriage alliance (rare) has severe reputation consequences

## Succession

When a chief dies:
1. **Eligible Heirs** are identified (matrilineal preference — sister's sons first)
2. Each heir has:
   - **Traits:** (Navigator, Warrior, Diplomat, Carver, Spiritual Leader, etc.)
   - **Moiety:** Same as deceased (inherited from mother)
   - **Age:** Younger heirs have more potential, less experience
   - **Alliances:** Who supports them
3. Player **chooses** among eligible heirs (not always the "best" — sometimes limited options create drama)
4. **Succession potlatch** validates the choice
5. Unselected heirs may cause problems (rivalry, departure) or become valuable advisors

## Lineage Display

The lineage screen shows:
- Family tree branching across generations
- Moiety color coding (Raven = dark purple/black, Eagle = white/gold)
- Marriage connections as horizontal links
- Prestige ratings per generation
- Active and historical chiefs highlighted
- Crest accumulation across the lineage

# 12. SPIRITUAL / OMEN SYSTEM

## Design Philosophy

Spirituality in Tidelines is atmospheric, respectful, and mechanically meaningful — but never cartoonish, never "mana bars," never casual. The spiritual world is present as a layer of guidance, mystery, and emotional depth. The player does not "cast spells." They **receive signs**, **seek guidance**, and **prepare themselves spiritually** for important undertakings.

## Omen Events

Omens appear as contextual events — things the chief or their advisors notice. They can occur:
- Before a major voyage
- During navigation (especially in fog or at night)
- At seasonal transitions
- After significant events (victory, death, potlatch)
- In dreams (between-season narrative moments)

### Omen Types

| Omen | Meaning | Mechanical Effect |
|------|---------|-------------------|
| **Eagle sighting at dawn** | Clarity, leadership | +scouting range for next voyage |
| **Raven calling from unusual direction** | Change is coming, be adaptable | Random modifier: could be good or bad |
| **Killer whale pod near village** | Ocean favor, protection | +seaworthiness for next voyage |
| **Frog chorus at dusk** | Abundance approaching, transition | +food harvest next season |
| **Bear seen near forest edge** | Strength needed, endurance | +defense bonus, possible threat incoming |
| **Unusual fog patterns** | Uncertainty, hidden things | Navigation event will occur |
| **Salmon arriving early/late** | Disrupted cycle | Resource forecast shift |
| **Dream of ancestor** | Guidance from the past | Reveals useful information (route, ally, threat) |
| **Eagle and raven seen together** | Unusual unity / balance | Diplomatic opportunity |
| **Storm petrel sighting** | Serious storm approaching | Weather warning, prepare or delay |

### Omen Interpretation

Not all omens are clear. The player can:
1. **Interpret personally** — choose from 2-3 possible meanings
2. **Consult spiritual advisor** — if one is assigned, they provide clearer interpretation (+accuracy)
3. **Ignore the omen** — no mechanical effect, but missing warnings has consequences

Wrong interpretations aren't punished harshly — they create interesting situations.

## Blessing System

Before major undertakings, the player can request a blessing:

### Pre-Voyage Blessing
- Ceremonial specialist performs ritual
- Costs: time + small resource investment
- Effect: +modifier to voyage safety, navigation, or encounter outcomes
- Visually: brief ceremonial animation, paddle dipping, smoke/incense

### Pre-Battle Blessing
- Warrior preparation ritual
- Costs: time + resources
- Effect: +morale, +intimidation for the encounter
- Cannot stack with pre-voyage blessing (choose one)

### Pole Blessing
- During pole-raising ceremony
- Built into the pole-raising event
- Effect: +prestige for the pole, spiritual significance modifier

### Seasonal Blessing
- At the start of a season (especially winter)
- Community-wide ritual
- Effect: General village morale and productivity bonus

## Spiritual Advisor

A named NPC role. Not every village has one at all times.
- **Recruitment:** May arrive as an event, or can be trained over time
- **Abilities:**
  - Better omen interpretation accuracy
  - Access to dream-sequence events between seasons
  - Pre-voyage blessings are more effective
  - Can sense danger approaching (early warning events)
- **Cost:** Must be supported, not assigned to labor, consumes some resources
- **Narrative Role:** Provides atmospheric dialogue, contextual wisdom, and emotional weight

## Dream Sequences

Between seasons (especially winter), the chief may have a dream:
- Presented as a brief narrative vignette with pixel art illustration
- The dream hints at upcoming events, threats, or opportunities
- Some dreams reference ancestors or pole imagery
- Dreams become more urgent/troubled in later eras
- The player makes a choice within the dream that may have subtle effects

Dreams are one of the most atmospheric features of the game — quiet, beautiful, mysterious moments that break up the strategic gameplay.

# 13. CONFLICT SYSTEM

## Design Philosophy

Conflict in Tidelines is **central, dangerous, and emotionally powerful**. Raids are not random nuisance events — they are campaigns that can define a chief's legacy, create generational feuds, and reshape the political map of the coast. Every conflict is a **multi-step operation** with decisions at each phase. Victory brings prestige, wealth, and fear. Defeat brings grief, obligation, and the long memory of enemies.

Conflict connects directly to the game's other pillars:
- **Prestige:** Victory in conflict is one of the fastest ways to gain (or lose) prestige
- **Potlatch:** Captured wealth fuels potlatch. Great victories demand commemorative feasts
- **Poles:** A war memorial pole immortalizes a battle. A shame pole punishes a coward
- **Lineage:** Warriors become ancestors. Chiefs who fall in defense become legends. Feuds pass to heirs
- **Spirituality:** Pre-battle blessings, war omens, and ancestral dreams shape conflict outcomes

There is **no graphic violence**. No dismemberment. No civilian abuse mechanics. Violence is treated with the gravity it deserves — outcomes are described in terms of victory, defeat, retreat, capture, loss, grief, and consequence. The tone is serious, historical, and emotionally weighty.

## Conflict Types

### 1. Defensive Village Raid
- Enemy war canoes approach your village
- **Warning phase:** Lookout spots sails (if watchtower exists: 2 turns warning; without: 1 turn or surprise)
- **Scramble phase:** Player allocates crew, positions defenses, decides strategy
- **Choices:** Meet at sea, defend the beach, retreat to longhouse, send a peace envoy, evacuate women and children to forest
- Resolution heavily favors the prepared. A surprised village is devastated

### 2. Offensive Raid Expedition
- Player **plans and launches** a multi-turn raid on a target
- **Planning:** Select objective, choose crew, load canoe, gather intelligence
- **Voyage:** Navigate to the target — weather, navigation events, crew morale all matter en route
- **Approach:** Choose stealth (night/fog) or intimidation (direct approach in formation)
- **Operation:** Execute raid objective (see Raid Objectives below)
- **Withdrawal:** Get home alive with your gains — pursuit possible
- **Aftermath:** Process captives, repair canoes, count losses, anticipate retaliation

### 3. Sea Encounter
- Hostile canoes encountered during open-water travel
- Can occur randomly, in contested waters, or as ambush during a feud
- **Fast-paced:** Fewer phases, quicker resolution. The sea itself is a factor
- **Chase mechanics:** Fleeing is a valid option — canoe speed and crew stamina determine escape
- **Boarding:** If canoes close, boarding actions occur (crew vs. crew, canoe-to-canoe)

### 4. Territorial Dispute
- Overlapping claims on fishing grounds, cedar groves, trade routes, or island camps
- **Escalation ladder:** Warning → Posturing → Confrontation → Skirmish → Full Conflict
- Can be resolved at any point through: diplomacy, intimidation, potlatch competition, marriage alliance, or force
- Unresolved disputes fester into feuds

### 5. Coalition War (Later Eras)
- Large-scale threat requiring multiple villages to respond
- Era 3-5: Tlingit expansion, Tsimshian competition, European-armed raiders
- **Requires:** Alliance building, war council events, coordinated fleet action
- **Unique mechanics:** Allied AI canoes fight alongside you, coalition politics, shared spoils disputes
- **Failure consequences:** Village destruction, forced tribute, loss of territory

### 6. Retaliatory Strike
- **Triggered automatically** when your village has been raided
- Community pressure to respond builds over turns. Ignoring it costs morale and prestige
- A retaliation raid has bonus morale but the enemy may be expecting you
- The cycle of raid and retaliation is how feuds begin

## Raid Objectives

Not all raids are the same. When planning an offensive raid, the player selects a **primary objective**:

| Objective | Description | Risk | Reward | Prestige |
|-----------|-------------|------|--------|----------|
| **Resource Seizure** | Raid for food, cedar, trade goods | Medium | High material | Low |
| **Canoe Capture** | Take enemy canoes beached on shore | High | Fleet expansion | Medium |
| **Prestige Raid** | Strike and withdraw — the point is humiliation | Medium | Low material | High |
| **Captive Taking** | Seize individuals for ransom negotiation | High | Diplomatic leverage | Medium |
| **Territorial Claim** | Occupy and hold a contested resource site | Very High | Long-term resource access | High |
| **Retaliation** | Punish a previous aggressor | Medium | Variable | High (restores lost honor) |

**Secondary objectives** can be added (at increased risk):
- Burn enemy canoes (denies future sea power)
- Destroy fish drying racks (economic damage)
- Steal crest objects (extreme insult — guaranteed feud escalation)
- Plant your crest marker (territorial claim + humiliation)

## Multi-Phase Operation System

Every significant conflict is resolved through a **multi-phase strategic operation**, not a single dice roll. Each phase presents meaningful choices and can change the outcome dramatically.

### Phase 1: Intelligence & Scouting (Offensive Only)
Before launching a raid, the player gathers information:
- **Send scouts:** A small canoe sent ahead. Takes 1 turn. Returns with target assessment
- **Consult traders:** Traders who visited the target may share layout, defenses, crew strength
- **Read omens:** Spiritual advisor may have visions about the outcome
- **Review feud history:** Previous encounters with this target affect crew morale and readiness

**Scouting Results** (quality depends on method and luck):
- Target's approximate crew strength (vague / accurate / precise)
- Defensive preparations (none seen / some fortification / heavily defended)
- Canoe count at beach (few / several / war fleet present)
- Alert level (unaware / cautious / expecting attack)

Poor intelligence leads to nasty surprises. Great intelligence enables ambush bonuses.

### Phase 2: Approach & Positioning
The raiding force is en route and nearing the target:
- **Time of day:** Dawn attack (surprise bonus), daylight (clear sight), dusk (concealment), night (maximum surprise but navigation risk)
- **Weather exploitation:** Fog provides cover. Storm masks sound but risks canoes. Clear skies mean you'll be spotted early
- **Formation:** Single canoe (stealthy), spread formation (intimidating), tight formation (mutual support)
- **Approach vector:** Open water (fastest, most visible), island-hopping (slower, uses cover), channel approach (confined but hidden)

**Intimidation Check** occurs here:
If the approaching force's **Intimidation Score** exceeds the defender's **Resolve Score**, the defender may:
- Flee before contact (rare — only if massively outmatched)
- Send a peace envoy (negotiation opportunity)
- Panic (defender morale penalty for the engagement)

### Phase 3: Engagement
The moment of contact. Player chooses their approach:

| Tactic | Description | Risk | Best When... |
|--------|-------------|------|-------------|
| **Overwhelming Assault** | Maximum force, direct attack | Very High | You have clear superiority |
| **Measured Push** | Steady advance, maintain formation | Medium | Uncertain about enemy strength |
| **Feint & Probe** | Test defenses, look for weakness | Low | Intelligence is poor |
| **Intimidation Display** | War cries, canoe posturing, crest display | Low | Your reputation is fearsome |
| **Encirclement** | Split force to attack from two directions | High | You have multiple canoes |
| **Negotiation Under Arms** | Approach with force, offer terms | Low-Medium | You want gains without losses |

**Defender Choices** (AI or player when defending):
- **Hold the beach** — strong defensive position but can be flanked
- **Meet at sea** — aggressive, uses canoe advantage
- **Fighting withdrawal** — trade ground for time, minimize losses
- **Concentrated defense** — protect the longhouse at all costs
- **Surrender terms** — offer tribute to avoid destruction

### Phase 4: Resolution
Calculated using the expanded **Force Score** system (see Technical Architecture). The resolution is NOT a single outcome — it unfolds in **rounds** (abstracted, not turn-by-turn micro-combat):

**Round structure:**
1. **Opening clash** — initial advantage based on surprise, positioning, morale
2. **Sustained engagement** — crew quality, stamina, and leadership matter most
3. **Turning point** — a critical moment where one side gains or loses momentum. May trigger a **Turning Point Event** (named warrior falls, chief rallies troops, canoe is captured, fire breaks out)
4. **Resolution** — one side breaks, withdraws, or achieves their objective

### Phase 5: Withdrawal & Pursuit
After resolution, the engagement isn't over:
- **Victor:** Must decide whether to pursue (risks overextension) or consolidate
- **Defeated:** Must manage retreat. Orderly withdrawal preserves crew. Rout means heavy losses
- **Pursuit mechanics:** Faster canoes can run down slower ones. Weather and tide affect pursuit
- **Rearguard action:** A brave crew member volunteering to cover retreat = powerful narrative moment + that character risks death

### Phase 6: Aftermath
The most consequential phase. What happens after the fighting stops shapes everything:

**Immediate Aftermath (same turn):**
- **Casualty report:** Named crew members killed, wounded, or captured are listed with narrative
- **Damage assessment:** Canoe condition, cargo status
- **Spoils division:** If victorious, captured goods are catalogued
- **Captive processing:** Captured individuals can be ransomed, adopted into your clan, or released as a gesture of magnanimity (each option has different prestige and diplomatic effects)

**Short-Term Aftermath (1-3 turns):**
- **Wound recovery:** Injured crew members need time. Some may never fully recover (permanent stat reduction)
- **Morale ripple:** Victory boosts morale across the village. Defeat creates fear and doubt
- **Reputation spread:** News of the conflict reaches other villages. Your **Conflict Reputation** updates
- **Retaliation clock:** If you raided someone, the retaliation timer starts ticking

**Long-Term Aftermath (seasons/eras):**
- **Feud state:** The relationship with the opposing community may enter an active feud
- **Legacy narrative:** The event becomes part of your chief's story, referenced in future events
- **Pole opportunity:** A significant victory unlocks the option to raise a War Memorial Pole
- **Song creation:** A great victory or heroic defense becomes a **battle song** (audio cue that plays in future conflicts with that enemy)
- **Ancestral memory:** If a named character died heroically, they may appear in future dream sequences as an ancestral guide

## Intimidation & Fear System

Intimidation is a **force multiplier** that can win conflicts before they start. It is built over time through actions, not simply assigned.

### Intimidation Score Components

| Component | Source | Value Range |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| **War Canoe Fleet** | Number and quality of war canoes | 0-25 |
| **Conflict Reputation** | Win/loss record, brutality of victories | 0-30 |
| **Chief's War Trait** | If chief has Warrior or Fearsome trait | 0-15 |
| **Crest Display** | War crests painted on canoes and shields | 0-10 |
| **Prestige Level** | General prestige of the village | 0-10 |
| **Recent Victory** | Won a conflict in the last 2 seasons | 0-10 |
| **War Song** | Battle song composed from past victory | 0-5 |
| **Total Maximum** | | **105** |

### Intimidation Effects

| Intimidation vs. Defender Resolve | Effect |
|-----------------------------------|--------|
| Score exceeds by 50+ | Defender may flee or surrender without contact |
| Score exceeds by 30-49 | Defender sends peace envoy; negotiation chance |
| Score exceeds by 15-29 | Defender morale penalty (-15 to -25) |
| Score exceeds by 1-14 | Minor defender morale penalty (-5 to -10) |
| Roughly equal (±14) | No intimidation effect |
| Defender exceeds by 15+ | Attacker morale penalty (defender is fearsome) |

### Resolve Score (Defender)
- Base resolve per community (larger/older = higher)
- Chief's Leadership trait bonus
- Defending home bonus (+20)
- Alliance strength bonus (nearby allies add resolve)
- Recent victory bonus
- Fortification bonus
- Desperation bonus (nothing left to lose = +15)

### Fear Accumulation
If a community is raided repeatedly:
- **First raid:** Normal response
- **Second raid (same feud):** Cautious. More defensive preparation
- **Third raid:** Fear builds. Morale penalties. Some community members want to negotiate
- **Fourth+ raid:** Community may seek alliance elsewhere, offer tribute, or launch desperate counter-raid
- Fear resets partially when the community wins a defensive engagement

## Feud System

Feuds are **persistent multi-generational conflicts** between communities. They are one of the most important systems in the game — a feud shapes diplomacy, trade, marriage, and conflict for decades.

### Feud Initiation
A feud begins when:
- A raid causes significant casualties or humiliation
- A captive is not ransomed within a reasonable time
- Crest objects are stolen (automatic feud)
- A potlatch shame pole is raised against the other community
- A marriage alliance is broken
- A territorial resource is seized by force

### Feud States

| State | Description | Effects |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| **GRIEVANCE** | Initial offense. Tension building | Trade penalty. Diplomatic options limited. Raiding chance +20% |
| **ACTIVE_FEUD** | Open hostility. Raids expected | No trade. No marriage. Allies pressured to choose sides. Retaliation raids auto-generated |
| **BLOOD_FEUD** | Deaths on both sides. Deep hatred | All above + feud events trigger each season. Named enemies emerge. Peace very difficult |
| **INHERITED_FEUD** | Previous generation's conflict continues | New chief can choose to continue or attempt peace. Community has strong opinions |
| **SMOULDERING** | Officially resolved but not forgotten | Minor trade penalty. Old warriors remember. Can reignite from small provocations |
| **RESOLVED** | Fully resolved through ceremony or marriage | No penalties. History remembered but no active effects |

### Feud Escalation
Each action in a feud pushes it up or down the escalation ladder:

**Escalating Actions (+escalation):**
- Killing named characters (+3)
- Stealing crest objects (+5 — maximum insult)
- Burning canoes or structures (+2)
- Refusing ransom demands (+2)
- Raising a shame pole (+4)
- Breaking a peace agreement (+5)

**De-escalating Actions (-escalation):**
- Returning captives (-2)
- Paying reparations / tribute (-3)
- Marriage alliance (-4)
- Joint potlatch (-3)
- Raising a reconciliation pole (-5)
- Time without incident (-1 per year of peace)

### Feud Resolution
To formally end an active feud:
1. **Reparation payment:** Goods proportional to damages inflicted
2. **Ceremony:** A formal peace ceremony (costs resources, time)
3. **Marriage alliance:** Seal the peace with an exogamous marriage
4. **Reconciliation pole:** Both communities contribute to a pole commemorating the peace
5. **Combined cost:** Usually requires 2+ of the above

Failed peace attempts (one side breaks the agreement) escalate the feud to BLOOD_FEUD immediately.

### Generational Feuds
When a chief dies during an active feud:
- The heir inherits the feud state
- Community members have opinions: some want revenge, some want peace
- The new chief can:
  - **Honor the feud** — continue hostilities (popular with warriors, costs resources)
  - **Seek peace** — attempt reconciliation (may anger warriors, costs prestige short-term)
  - **Escalate** — launch a major raid to end the feud decisively (very high risk)
- An inherited feud that the new chief resolves through diplomacy is a major prestige event

## War Traits

Chiefs and named crew members can develop **war traits** through conflict experience. These are permanent character modifiers.

### Chief War Traits

| Trait | Earned By | Effect |
|-------|-----------|--------|
| **Battle-Tested** | Surviving 3+ conflicts | +10% crew morale in conflict |
| **Fearsome** | Winning 3+ conflicts decisively | +15 Intimidation Score |
| **Raider** | Successfully completing 3+ offensive raids | +10% raid objective success rate |
| **Defender of the People** | Repelling 2+ village raids | +20% defensive fortification bonus |
| **Sea Wolf** | Winning 2+ sea encounters | +15% sea engagement advantage |
| **Peacemaker** | Resolving 2+ feuds diplomatically | +20% negotiation success; unlocks unique peace options |
| **Reckless** | Losing a conflict after choosing Overwhelming Assault | -5% crew morale; +5% attack power (troops fear and respect) |

| Trait | Earned By | Effect |
|-------|-----------|--------|
| **Veteran** | Surviving 2+ conflicts | +5 combat quality |

| **Loyal to the Death** | Volunteered for rearguard action and survived | +10 morale (personal); will never desert |

## Connection to Other Systems

### Conflict → Potlatch
- Captured wealth from raids is prime potlatch material
- A victory potlatch is expected after a major win (celebrating without sharing offends)
- Distributing spoils through potlatch converts material gain into lasting prestige
- A peace potlatch resolving a feud is one of the most impressive ceremonies possible

### Conflict → Poles
- **War Memorial Pole:** Commemorates a great victory. Specific crest figures represent the battle
- **Shame Pole (Conflict):** Erected after an enemy breaks a peace agreement or refuses ransom
- **Reconciliation Pole:** Raised jointly after a feud is resolved. Unique dual-crest design
- **Legacy Pole:** A chief who was a great warrior or defender has conflict scenes on their legacy pole

### Conflict → Lineage
- Named warriors who die become ancestors referenced in dream sequences
- Children of great warriors may inherit combat aptitude traits
- A chief's conflict record is part of their lineage display
- Feuds are visible on the lineage screen as red connection lines between families

### Conflict → Spirituality
- Pre-battle blessings from the spiritual advisor are critical (+10% combat modifier)
- War omens can appear before conflict (eagle circling = good omen; raven on dead tree = warning)
- Dreaming of a fallen warrior before a battle = +morale, +resolve
- Failing to properly honor the dead after battle = spiritual penalty, morale loss

## Era-Based Conflict Escalation

Conflict changes dramatically across the five eras:

### Era 1 — Flourishing Villages
- Conflicts are **rare and small** — occasional resource dispute, minor raid
- Primarily resolved through intimidation, negotiation, or single skirmishes
- Consequences are limited. Feuds resolve quickly
- Purpose: Teach the conflict system gently

### Era 2 — Expansion & Rivalry
- Conflicts become **regular and strategic** — territorial competition intensifies
- Full raid expeditions become available
- Feuds can persist across seasons
- First war canoe commissions
- Purpose: Conflict becomes a meaningful strategic tool

### Era 3 — Golden Age & First Contact
- **Peak conflict complexity** — large-scale raids, coalition warfare, complex feuds
- External threats appear (Tlingit expansion, unfamiliar raiders)
- European ships arrive — not yet hostile, but disrupting trade patterns and alliances
- War canoe fleets, alliance networks, and intimidation scores matter enormously
- Purpose: Conflict is woven into every strategic decision

### Era 4 — Pressure & Resistance
- **Existential conflict** — European colonizers, armed traders, disease-weakened communities
- Traditional warfare less effective against muskets and cannon
- New defensive strategies required: coalition, negotiation, strategic retreat
- Feuds between Indigenous communities become dangerous — division weakens everyone
- Purpose: Conflict shifts from rivalry to survival

### Era 5 — Endurance & Legacy
- **Conflict is suppressed** — colonial authorities ban warfare, confiscate canoes
- Resistance is subtle: legal challenges, cultural preservation, refusal to submit
- Old feuds may be forcibly "resolved" by colonial courts (unsatisfying, unjust)
- The conflict system transforms into a resistance system
- Purpose: The weight of everything the player built is tested

## Defensive Preparation

Players can invest in village defense across multiple layers:

### Outer Defense (Early Warning)
- **Watchtower:** Provides 2-turn warning of approaching canoes. Upgradeable for range
- **Scout canoe patrol:** A small canoe on patrol adds additional warning. Costs crew assignment
- **Alliance signal fires:** If allied with nearby communities, they may warn you of approaching threats

### Beach Defense (First Contact)
- **Beach fortifications:** Log barriers, sharpened stakes slow beach landings. Attackers take morale penalty
- **Hidden canoe launch:** A concealed channel allows flanking counterattack from behind attackers
- **Beach ambush positions:** Pre-positioned crew behind cover. Surprise bonus if triggered
- **Fire preparation:** Oil-soaked logs ready to ignite. Creates barrier and panic (non-lethal area denial)

### Village Defense (Last Stand)
- **Longhouse barricade:** Final defensive position. High defense bonus but you've already lost the beach
- **Escape route:** Hidden forest path for evacuating non-combatants. Preserves future even in defeat
- **Cache hideout:** Buried supplies that survive even if the village is looted

### Warrior Training
- **Regular training:** 1 turn/season dedicated to combat drills. Slow but steady improvement
- **War chief advisor:** A named NPC who trains warriors faster and unlocks advanced tactics
- **Veteran integration:** Experienced warriors automatically train newer crew members

## Morale System

Morale is a **critical combat modifier** that reflects the emotional and psychological state of your people. It operates on a scale of 0-100.

### Morale Modifiers

**Strong Positive (+15 to +25):**
- Defending your own village (homeland bonus)
- Recent potlatch hosted (+15 for 2 seasons)
- Spiritual blessing active
- Chief has Battle-Tested or Fearsome trait
- Avenging a previous raid (retaliation bonus)

**Moderate Positive (+5 to +14):**
- Recent victory (decays over 3 seasons)
- Well-fed (food surplus)
- Strong chief (high prestige)
- Allied reinforcements present

**Neutral (0):**
- Baseline state, no strong modifiers

**Moderate Negative (-5 to -14):**
- Far from home (distance penalty)
- Low food supplies
- Minor recent defeat
- Uncertain intelligence about the enemy
- Wounded crew members present

**Strong Negative (-15 to -25):**
- Recent devastating defeat
- Named character recently killed
- Succession crisis at home
- Epidemic affecting crew
- Third+ raid in the same feud (fear accumulation)
- Fighting former allies or relatives (marriage connections)

### Morale Breaking
If morale drops below **20** during an engagement:
- Crew may refuse orders (engagement choice overridden)
- Retreat becomes automatic
- Rout possible (crew scatters — heavy losses, canoe potentially abandoned)
- Named characters with **Loyal to the Death** trait resist breaking

### Morale Recovery
- **Victory:** +20 immediate, +10 sustained
- **Potlatch:** +15 sustained (lasts 2 seasons)
- **Spiritual ceremony:** +10
- **Rest at home (no conflict for 1 season):** +5
- **New chief with high prestige:** +10 (succession morale reset)
- **Feud resolution:** +15 (relief and hope)

# 14. HISTORICAL ERA CAMPAIGN

## ERA 1 — FLOURISHING VILLAGES (Generations 1-2)

**Narrative Theme:** *"The world is full and the sea provides."*

**Tone:** Hopeful, foundational, learning. The Pacific coast is a rich and complex world. Your village is one of many in a thriving network of communities.

**Gameplay Focus:**
- Learn core systems: village management, fishing, canoe use
- Build your first significant structures
- Establish your village identity (first crests, first small poles)
- Make initial alliances through marriage and trade
- First small-scale conflicts (territorial fishing disputes, minor raids)
- Experience the seasonal rhythm: spring preparation, summer abundance, autumn harvest, winter ceremony

**Key Events:**
- First canoe construction
- First major fishing season
- First trade voyage
- First encounter with a rival
- First seasonal ceremony
- First marriage alliance

**Win Condition to Advance:** Reach a village prestige threshold + successfully host a significant feast potlatch.

**Music/Visual Tone:** Warm, luminous. Golden summer light. Rich green forests. Calm waters. Learning and discovery.

---

## ERA 2 — EXPANDING INFLUENCE (Generations 2-3)

**Narrative Theme:** *"Our name is known beyond the inlet."*

**Tone:** Ambitious, competitive, growing. The player's community reaches beyond its home waters. Longer voyages, larger trades, deeper rivalries.

**Gameplay Focus:**
- Build or acquire a war canoe
- Launch longer trade expeditions (multi-day voyages)
- Navigate more complex diplomacy with distant groups (Tsimshian, Tlingit contacts)
- Commission and raise your first major pole
- Host a larger potlatch with regional guests
- Face a significant conflict or rivalry that tests your strength
- First marriage alliances with distant communities
- Clan/moiety dynamics become more important

**Key Events:**
- First long-distance voyage
- First encounter with a powerful rival chief
- First pole-raising ceremony
- First major potlatch
- A significant conflict (raid defense or offensive)
- An important marriage alliance

**Win Condition to Advance:** Multiple poles standing + significant prestige level + control of key trade routes or resources.

**Music/Visual Tone:** Epic, swelling. Dramatic ocean crossings. Fog-shrouded arrivals at distant villages. Firelit ceremonies with large crowds. More complex, layered compositions.

---

## ERA 3 — GREAT LEGACY (Generation 3-4)

**Narrative Theme:** *"We are among the greatest houses on the coast."*

**Tone:** Peak power, defining choices, high stakes. This is the era of the grandest potlatches, the most impressive poles, the most powerful canoes, and the most consequential decisions.

**Gameplay Focus:**
- Build prestige canoe (ultimate vessel)
- Plan and execute the Great Potlatch (largest possible ceremony)
- Multiple poles, each unique, defining the village skyline
- Peak diplomatic network — alliances, rivalries, obligations
- Defining conflict: a major confrontation that shapes the region
- Master carver at peak, producing the finest work
- Leadership choices: what kind of legacy are you building? Diplomatic? Martial? Ceremonial? Spiritual?
- Copper acquisition and redistribution (highest-value prestige acts)

**Key Events:**
- Acquisition of a great copper
- The Great Potlatch
- A defining battle or diplomatic triumph
- A succession crisis (choosing the next generation's leader)
- A spiritual encounter of unusual significance
- The raising of a masterwork pole

**Win Condition to Advance:** Triggered by time + era-specific events (the world begins to change regardless of player action).

**Music/Visual Tone:** Grand, majestic, but with undercurrents of tension. Sunset palettes. Massive ceremony scenes. The most elaborate visual moments in the game. A sense that this peak cannot last.

---

## ERA 4 — DISRUPTION AND SURVIVAL (Generation 4-5)

**Narrative Theme:** *"The world we knew is changing. We must endure."*

**Tone:** Somber, tense, emotionally heavy. Historical pressures arrive — epidemic disease, demographic loss, new economic pressures, outside powers, resource competition, forced change. The player cannot "win" against history — they must navigate it.

**Gameplay Focus:**
- Epidemic event: devastating population loss (handled respectfully, not graphically)
  - Village shrinks visibly
  - Some named characters die
  - Labor force reduced
  - Emotional narrative moments
- Resource pressures: cedar becoming scarce, fishing grounds contested by new actors
- External powers arrive: trading vessels, missionaries, economic disruption
- Economic shift: new trade goods (metal tools, etc.) change the economy
- Demographic consolidation: possibly merging with allied villages
- Preservation choices: which traditions are maintained? Which buildings are kept? Which poles are protected?
- Defensive diplomacy becomes critical
- Some former allies weaken or disappear

**Key Events:**
- The epidemic arrives (major narrative/emotional event)
- First contact with external traders/missionaries
- Loss of a key ally (village abandoned due to population loss)
- Economic disruption event
- A choice about whether to consolidate villages
- A preservation moment: protecting a pole, a ceremony, a canoe tradition

**Emotional Note:** This era should be handled with extreme care. It represents real historical trauma. The design must balance gameplay engagement with emotional respect. The player should feel the weight of loss without the game becoming exploitative or hopeless.

**Music/Visual Tone:** Muted palette. Grayer skies. Rain more frequent. Fewer people in village scenes. Quieter, more melancholic music. Occasional moments of beauty and resilience break through the somber tone — a ceremony held despite everything, a canoe launched with a smaller crew, a pole still standing against the grey sky.

---

## ERA 5 — ENDURING MEMORY (Endgame / Epilogue)

**Narrative Theme:** *"What remains when the tide recedes?"*

**Tone:** Reflective, elegiac, ultimately hopeful. The final era is shorter — more epilogue than full gameplay phase. It asks the player: what did you build? What survives? What is remembered?

**Gameplay Focus:**
- Final succession: choosing the last playable chief
- Last acts: one more ceremony? one more pole? one more voyage?
- Legacy assessment: the game reviews all major decisions, poles, potlatches, alliances
- Village state: what does it look like now? What stands? What has changed?
- Lineage review: which families thrived? Which alliances held?

**Legacy Scoring:**

The endgame score is calculated across multiple dimensions:

| Dimension | What Counts |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Poles Standing** | Number, quality, variety of poles created |
| **Potlatches Hosted** | Number, scale, and impressiveness of ceremonies |
| **Generosity Record** | Total wealth redistributed over the campaign |
| **Lineage Strength** | Surviving family members, alliance network |
| **Canoe Legacy** | Greatest canoes built, farthest voyages completed |
| **Territorial Memory** | Routes discovered, sites maintained, camps established |
| **Diplomatic Web** | Alliances formed, peace achieved, marriages arranged |
| **Spiritual Harmony** | Omens heeded, blessings sought, ceremonies honored |
| **Resilience** | How the community navigated disruption and loss |
| **Cultural Continuity** | Traditions maintained through difficulty |

**The Final Screen:**
A slow pan across the village as it exists at game end. Each pole is visible, labeled with what it commemorates. A narrative summary of the lineage's history scrolls. The ocean is always present. The music is bittersweet but ultimately affirming: *this happened. It mattered. It is remembered.*

**Music/Visual Tone:** Dawn light after storm. Peaceful water. The village as it is — perhaps smaller, perhaps changed, but present. The poles stand. The music resolves gently. Credits roll over a tide coming in.

# 19. MVP PLAN — VERTICAL SLICE

## Goal
Prove the core fantasy is compelling in a single playable experience of approximately 30-45 minutes.

## Scope: "One Year on the Coast"

The vertical slice covers one in-game year (4 seasons) with one chief, one village, and a limited but complete map.

### Included Systems (Simplified)

1. **Village Management (Basic)**
   - 3 buildings: Longhouse, Canoe House, Smoke House
   - 3 labor roles: Fisher, Builder, Carver
   - Simple food economy: fish → smoke → store
   - One canoe construction project

2. **Canoe Navigation (Core)**
   - One canoe type (Travel Canoe)
   - Simple map with: home village, fishing ground, trade village, open-water crossing
   - Basic weather system (clear, rain, fog)
   - Tidal mechanic on one narrow passage
   - 3-4 sea encounter events

3. **Trade (Basic)**
   - One trade destination
   - Simple goods exchange
   - One marriage alliance opportunity

4. **One Potlatch**
   - End-of-year ceremony
   - Accumulate food and goods → invite neighbors → distribute → prestige result
   - Simplified gift allocation

5. **One Pole**
   - Commission a simple pole mid-year
   - See carving progress
   - Raise it during the potlatch season
   - It stands in the village afterward

6. **3-4 Events**
   - Fog navigation challenge
   - Killer whale omen
   - Fishing ground dispute
   - One spiritual/dream event

7. **One Conflict**
   - Small raid encounter (sea-based)
   - Simplified tactical resolution
   - Prestige/material consequences

### NOT Included in MVP
- Full lineage/succession system
- Multiple canoe types
- Era transitions
- Complex diplomacy
- Full marriage system
- Multiple pole types

### Success Criteria
The MVP proves:
- ✅ Canoe navigation feels good and atmospheric
- ✅ Village management creates meaningful choices
- ✅ The potlatch-as-prestige mechanic works and feels novel
- ✅ A pole can be commissioned, carved, and raised with emotional impact
- ✅ The art style evokes the Pacific Northwest coast beautifully
- ✅ The tone is reverent, strategic, and engaging
- ✅ A player wants to keep playing after the vertical slice ends

# APPENDICES

## Appendix A: Cultural Consultation Note

This game draws inspiration from Haida history, culture, and worldview. It is essential that development includes:
- Consultation with Haida cultural advisors or knowledge keepers
- Review of cultural representation at key milestones
- Willingness to modify or remove elements that are inappropriate
- Revenue-sharing or partnership consideration
- Clear framing in-game that this is *inspired by* rather than a definitive representation

This is not optional. It is a core design requirement.
